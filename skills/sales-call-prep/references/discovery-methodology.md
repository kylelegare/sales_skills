# Discovery Methodology Reference

Distilled from established B2B discovery and question-based-discovery practice. This is not a skill — it's the knowledge that the call prep skill loads to generate great questions.

## The Discovery Depth Chain

Discovery has 4 phases. Each builds on the last. Don't skip ahead.

1. **Business Problem** — Surface a specific suffering metric the decision maker owns
2. **Cause Analysis** — Understand WHY the problem is happening (root causes)
3. **Negative Impact** — Explore the ripple effects, cost of inaction, second-order consequences
4. **Future State** — Build buying criteria that favor your solution before you present it

Within each phase: always ask an **open** question first, then **targeted** questions. Open earns you psychological permission to go targeted.

---

## Opening the Call

### Upfront Contract (Rules of Engagement)

Set 3 things in the first 5 minutes:
1. **Objective** — "The outcome I recommend we shoot for is..."
2. **Agenda** — "Here's what I had in mind: first 10-15 minutes understanding your challenges, then I'll share how we might help"
3. **Decision** — "By the end, we'll both be in a position to decide: schedule a next step, or go our separate ways"

Close with: **"Does that feel fair?"**

Use softening language: "Here's what I'm thinking. Let me know if you have a different agenda."

### Choosing Your Opening Technique

**Inbound / actively exploring buyer** — Go-Back-In-Time:
1. "Tell me about what motivated you to look into [solution category]."
2. "What are you aiming to accomplish with something like this?"
3. "Do you mind if I go back in time with you for a second? What was the original challenge that caused you to prioritize this?"

**Outbound / latent pain buyer** — Context-Led Question:
- "[Name], I noticed [observation from research]. [Why it's relevant]. [Question that ties to a problem you solve]."

**Cold / resistant buyer** — Discovery Prompter:
1. Pain statement about a similar customer
2. Expand on their pain
3. Negative impact they experienced
4. Short solution outcome (brief!)
5. Pass the torch: "Enough about us. Help me understand the challenges you're facing in [area] that would be problematic if you didn't solve them 6-12 months out."

## Phase 1: Surfacing Problems

### The Problem Formula
1. Share context ("we speak with teams like yours daily...")
2. Share insight ("they tell us they struggle with X, Y, Z...")
3. Ask open-ended tough question ("curious to what extent any of these resonate...")
4. Optional humbling disclaimer ("just looking to understand how you operate")

### Humbling Disclaimers
Use BEFORE tough questions to reduce resistance:
- "I'm not sure how to ask this, but..."
- "At the risk of being direct, I'm curious..."
- "I may be out of bounds here, yet I'm curious..."
- "I could be completely wrong about this..."

Use AFTER tough questions to soften the landing:
- "Just looking to understand how you operate"
- "Really curious to understand how you think about it"
- "Again, not sure how to ask that"

### "To What Extent" Pattern
Turns any closed question into an open one. Use everywhere.
- BAD: "Is this a problem?" → GOOD: "To what extent has this been a gap for your team?"
- BAD: "Does this solve your problem?" → GOOD: "To what extent does this solve your problem?"
- "To what extent have you discussed these topics internally?"
- "To what extent have you tried addressing this on your own, or has it not been that important?"

### Negative Disposition (the "or no" technique)
End questions with a negative option. Gives the prospect permission to be honest.
- "...or would you say that's pretty much all figured out?"
- "...or has it not been that relevant enough to address until now?"
- "...or would you say there is no real impact?"
- "...or does that almost never happen?"
- "...or do you think I'm off base?"

### Recapping (Discovery-Depth: "Conversational Grease")
After 3-4 questions, pause and summarize using their exact words:
- "So if I understand you correctly, it sounds like [their words]. Am I understanding that correctly?"

The recap with assumption ("it sounds like...") is especially powerful — whether your assumption is right or wrong, the prospect will elaborate or correct you. Both give you more information.

Then transition: "Great. Now that we're aligned on that, I'm curious about what's driving this..."

## Phase 2: Cause Analysis

### Open Diagnostic Question (ask first, always)
- "What's your opinion on why [business problem] is happening?"

### Targeted Diagnostic Questions (after the open question)
- **"To what extent" format:** "To what extent is [root cause] an issue for you?"
- **Context-led format:** "Many of our customers struggle with [root cause]. How is that showing up in your world?"

### Root Cause Map (mental model)
For each customer problem you solve:
- What root cause does your capability address?
- What targeted question surfaces that root cause?
- Especially: what root cause does ONLY your product address? (competitive moat)

### Classify What You Hear
- **Business problem** = a suffering metric the decision maker owns (close rates dropped from 25% to 20%)
- **Root cause** = a behavior or symptom that leads to the suffering metric (reps skip discovery)
- **Desired solution** = buyer expresses needs with implied action ("we need better training")

If the buyer leads with a root cause, peel back: "What impact is that having on the business?" to get to the metric.

## Phase 3: Negative Impact

### Summarize and Transition
Summarize both the business problem AND root causes before exploring impact:
- "I'm asking this because your CFO is probably going to care about it, but what metric would improve the most if we solved this?"

### Quantify Pain
- Identify the "metric that matters"
- Calculate the gap between current and desired state
- Translate to a monthly cost of inaction (monthly = persistent pressure)

### Open Negative Impact Questions
- "What are the ripple effects you're seeing this have on the rest of the business?"
- "What would get derailed if you didn't make progress on this?"
- "Who else does this challenge impact, and how?"

### Targeted Negative Impact Questions
- "To what extent is [specific consequence] showing up as a result of this?"
- "Many of our customers dealing with [problem] were concerned about [consequence]. To what extent is that the case with you?"

Only need 2-3 targeted negative impact questions. Don't overdo it.

### Impact Sequence
1. "Do you have a specific example of when this last happened?"
2. "How often would you say that happens?"
3. "To what extent is this happening across other members of your team, or just you?"
4. "Have you tried solving for it in the past, or has it not been that big of a deal?"
5. "If we can help solve for it, is it worth talking more?"

## Phase 4: Future State & Buying Criteria

### Open Future State Question
- "What do you think you need to solve this challenge?"

### Permission Transition
- "Can I try a few additional ideas on you?" (nobody ever says no)

### Targeted Solution Questions
- "Imagine being able to [capability]. To what degree would that move the needle on this problem?"
- "How important would it be to you to [capability]?"
- Reference their root cause: "One of the things you shared is [root cause]. To what extent is it gonna be important that [capability addressing it]?"

Goal: buyer agrees that what you have is what they need BEFORE you show it.

### Bring the Future to the Present
- "If we end up showing you what you're looking for, what happens after that?"
- "Pretending you like our walkthrough and believe we can help, what would happen next?"
- "If your boss likes what she sees, what would happen after that?"

## Listening & Follow-Up Patterns

### Four Listening Triggers
1. **Prospect explicitly admits a problem** → "Can you tell me more about that?"
2. **Prospect hints at a problem** → "How do you mean?"
3. **Prospect asks YOU a question** → "Sounds like you're asking that for a reason."
4. **Prospect compliments your offering** → "Where do you find this most beneficial?" (then silence)

### Getting Longer Answers
- "Can you help me understand..."
- "Can you walk me through..."
- "Talk to me about..."

### Empathetic Statements (unlock truth without asking)
- "It sounds like you're frustrated with..."
- "It seems like this is really important to you..."
- "It looks like you're having trouble with..."
Then: SILENCE. No "I" in these — always "you."

### Calling Out the Prospect (when things feel off)
- "I could be way off, but I'm getting the sense this is not so interesting for you at this point. Am I way off?"
- "I could be wrong here, yet it seems like although you like our offering, you feel like you'll be ok without us..."
- "It sounds like you guys are really excellent at XYZ and there's no room for improvement. Is that crazy for me to say?"

## Closing Motion Essentials

### Qualify Vendor of Choice
- "If price were identical, who would you choose to move forward with today?"

### Map the Remaining Path (progressive intrusion — least to most sensitive)
1. **Steps:** "What still needs to happen before we move forward together?" + "What comes after that?"
2. **People:** "Who is involved in each of those steps?"
3. **Timeline:** "How much time between each step?"
4. **Money:** "To what extent is there risk of not being able to secure the funding?"

### Spot-Check Risks
- "I didn't hear you mention [legal/security/procurement]. Is that going to come up?"

### Stay Current
Open every interaction with: "Before we get into the agenda, what's changed since last we spoke?"

---

## Question Quality Checklist

Before generating any question, check:
- Is it grounded in a specific gap from the deal review or dossier? (not generic)
- Does it follow open-before-targeted sequencing?
- Does it use softening language or a humbling disclaimer if it's a tough question?
- Does it end with a negative disposition option if it's a direct question?
- Is it in the buyer's language, not yours?
- Would this question pass the "would I actually say this out loud?" test?
